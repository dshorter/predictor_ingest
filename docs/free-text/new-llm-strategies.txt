3. Does methodology align with accepted principles? Are we using LLMs cleverly?
What aligns well with accepted practice:

Velocity/novelty/bridge as trend signals — this maps to established bibliometric and social network analysis approaches. Velocity is analogous to citation burst detection (Kleinberg bursts). Novelty tracks entity age. Bridge score approximates betweenness centrality. These are sound primitives.

Source diversity for corroboration — this is textbook information science. Single-source signals are noise; multi-source convergence is signal. The methodology correctly identifies this.

Escalation architecture — using a cheap model first and escalating to expensive model on quality gate failure is a well-established pattern (cascade classification). The quality gates (evidence fidelity, orphan detection) are CPU-based and zero-cost, which is smart.

Archive-first — storing raw content and re-extracting as schemas improve is sound data engineering.

What could be more clever with LLMs:

Right now the LLM does one thing: structured extraction (entities + relations from a single article). This is the most expensive and least creative use. Consider:

Cross-document synthesis — after extracting N articles, feed the extracted structured data (not raw text) back to an LLM and ask: "Which entity clusters are forming? What patterns do you see across these 25 articles?" This is cheap (structured JSON input is token-efficient) and produces higher-order insight that pure graph math misses.

Relation inference — the current pipeline only extracts explicitly stated relations. But film trade press is full of implicit relations (an article about Sundance selections implicitly connects every listed film to Sundance, every director to their film, etc.). A second LLM pass on the extraction output could infer these, marked as kind: "inferred".

Entity disambiguation — rather than fuzzy string matching in src/resolve/, use an LLM to resolve ambiguous entities. "Jordan Peele" vs "Peele" vs "Jordan" in a film context is trivial for an LLM but hard for string matching. This could be batched cheaply.

Trend narrative generation — after computing trend scores, have an LLM explain why an entity is trending by reading its evidence snippets. This makes the graph output actionable rather than just visual.

Idea 2: Relation Inference (Implicit Relations)
The problem it solves:

Look at the data: 98.9% of relations are asserted, only 1.1% are inferred. This is by design — the extraction prompt says "only use semantic relations when evidence supports them." But film trade press is loaded with implicit relations that are never stated explicitly:

An article listing Sundance 2026 selections mentions 15 films. The extraction might get SELECTED_BY for a few, but the bulk relationship (every listed film → SELECTED_BY → Sundance) is often partially extracted.

A profile piece about a producer mentions their filmography in passing. The LLM extracts the people but the PRODUCES relations are implicit from context, not stated as "X produced Y."

A casting announcement says "Jordan Peele's next film has cast Lupita Nyong'o." The extraction gets STARS_IN but may miss DIRECTS (Jordan Peele → the film) because it's possessive, not a verb.

Your data proves this: 38.3% of entities are islands — they have zero semantic relations (only MENTIONS). These are entities the LLM correctly identified as notable but couldn't find explicit relation evidence for. That's 839 entities contributing nothing to the graph topology.

The problem it solves:
